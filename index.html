01 / Identity

Two languages, one precision.

I'm Dishant M Parmar — an accountant who codes, and a developer who still closes his books by hand. By day I keep ledgers, reconcile accounts, and translate a business into the language of numbers. By night I build interfaces, query databases, and ship code that someone, somewhere, will rely on.

I hold a BCA in Computer Applications alongside my accounting practice. The two were never separate for me — both reward patience, both punish guesswork, and both come down to the same question: does it balance?

GST returns, TDS, bank reconciliation, final accounts. I work mostly with small businesses — the kind where the owner walks in with a shoebox of receipts and walks out with a balance sheet they actually understand.

Practice · Night

HTML, CSS, vanilla JavaScript on the front. PHP and MySQL on the back. I avoid heavy frameworks unless the project demands them — most websites are better served by a few well-written files than a build pipeline that takes forty seconds to start.

06 / Process

Four steps, in order.

01

Read everything

Before I write a line of code or open a ledger, I read the brief twice. I read the existing books. I read what the client actually does, not what they say they do. Most errors are made here, before the work starts.

02

Name things honestly

Variables, ledger heads, files, routes — every name says what it is, not what I hoped it would be. sundry_creditors_v2_final is a lie. outstanding_payables_march_2025 is the truth.

03

Test the edge cases

Negative numbers. Leap years. Empty form fields. A user who types "two thousand" instead of 2000. I find the boundary of every system before someone else does, because they always do.

04

Ship when it balances

The trial balance has to foot. The build has to pass. The client has to be able to use it without me on a Sunday. When those three are true, we ship. Not before.
